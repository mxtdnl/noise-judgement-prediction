🕵️ The Briefing Room

You are the intelligence chief who carries analysts' probability estimates to the President — and every choice about how precisely to report them can make or break a decision. Can you tell when granularity is signal, and when it's just decoration?

How to play


  • Your job: each round, an analyst hands you a probability estimate. A decision hangs on it — the President acts only if the probability they hear clears a stated threshold.
  • Your one move: choose how to report the estimate — as a vague phrase (free), rounded to the nearest 10% (costs 1 point of briefing time), or the exact figure (costs 2 points).
  • Vague phrases are interpreted, not read: the President mentally converts "a serious possibility" into some number — and different listeners land anywhere in a wide band (Sherman Kent found 20%–80% for that exact phrase).
  • Analysts differ: a superforecaster's digits are real signal — 63% genuinely means 63%. A regular analyst's estimate is honest but fuzzy: the truth may sit ±14 points away, so their final digits are mostly noise.
  • Scoring: +12 if the President's decision matches what the true probability warranted, +3 bonus if you chose the reporting mode with the best expected payoff, minus the briefing cost. Max 112. Your best total is saved on this device.
  • The skill: pay for precision only when it buys you something — a skilled source and an estimate near the decision threshold. Everywhere else, precision is money down the drain.
